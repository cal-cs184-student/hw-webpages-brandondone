Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

  1. Experiment with some of the parameters in the simulation. To do so, pause the simulation at the start with P, modify the values of interest, and then resume by pressing P again. You can also restart the simulation at any time from the cloth's starting position by pressing R. Describe the effects of changing the spring constant ks; how does the cloth behave from start to rest with a very low ks? A high ks?

    With a very low ks, the cloth behaves loosely and stretches easily. From start to rest it sags much farther, the grid deforms more visibly, and the cloth can look rubbery because neighboring point masses are not pulled strongly back toward their rest lengths. It can also take longer to settle because it overshoots and flops around more.

    With a high ks, the cloth becomes much stiffer. It keeps its original shape better, stretches less, and comes to rest with less sag. If ks is pushed extremely high, the simulation can become jittery or unstable because each timestep produces much larger spring forces.

  2. What about for density?

    Higher density makes the cloth effectively heavier. Gravity accelerates all masses equally, but the spring forces have less influence relative to the larger mass, so the cloth stretches more, sags lower, and responds more sluggishly. It tends to look heavier and more droopy overall.

    Lower density makes the cloth feel lighter and more spring-dominated. It resists sagging more, rebounds more quickly, and generally looks tighter and more lively than the default setup.

  3. What about for damping?

    Low damping means the cloth loses very little energy, so it swings, bounces, and oscillates for a long time before coming to rest. Compared to the default, it looks more energetic and can feel less realistic if it keeps moving for too long.

    High damping removes energy quickly. The cloth overshoots less, settles faster, and generally moves in a more subdued way. If the damping is set very high, the motion can start to feel thick or sluggish, almost as if the cloth is moving through syrup.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

  1. Describe your implementation of handling collisions with spheres and planes.

    For spheres, I check whether a point mass's current position is inside or on the sphere by comparing its distance from the sphere origin to radius. If a collision occurs, I compute the corresponding point on the sphere surface along the same radial direction, then move the point mass from last_position toward that surface point scaled by (1 - friction). This keeps the mass just outside the sphere while friction reduces sliding.

    For planes, I compute signed distances to the plane for both last_position and the current position. If the signs show that the point crossed the plane during the timestep, I project it back onto the plane, offset it slightly above the surface using SURFACE_OFFSET, and again move from last_position toward that corrected point scaled by (1 - friction).

    I also updated Cloth::simulate so that every point mass is tested against every collision object during each timestep.
